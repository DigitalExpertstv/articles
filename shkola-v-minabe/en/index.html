The Minab School: What We Know and What We Don't

George Ryzhenko | March 2026

This is about a girls' school in the city of Minab in southern Iran, 22 km from the Strait of Hormuz. It's currently at the center of one of the war's biggest information battles — and a massive meltdown across the internet.

Disclaimer. The death of children is always terrible. Children are certainly not to blame for anything. Not in Iran, not in Russia, not in Israel. But they're also not to blame for the fact that the Iranian regime spent two decades working on producing nuclear weapons while openly promising to destroy the "Zionist entity." Israel is a small country. A one-nuke country.

George Ryzhenko

Producer, entrepreneur

What happened and who struck

The strike was carried out on February 28, within the first half hour of the operation. The official target was the Sayyid al-Shuhada military complex, the headquarters of the IRGC Navy's Asef Missile Brigade — one of the main strike units of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps controlling the Strait of Hormuz.

According to a Reuters investigation published on March 6, American military investigators are leaning toward the conclusion that the strike was likely carried out by US forces — against an IRGC military target. Neither the US nor Israel has officially claimed responsibility.

Secretary of Defense Hegseth:

«We're investigating that. We, of course, never target civilian targets»

Secretary of State Rubio:

«The Department of War would be investigating if that was our strike»

White House Press Secretary Caroline Leavitt:

«The Iranian regime targets civilians and children — not the United States of America»

An IDF spokesperson stated that Israel is unaware of any Israeli strike in the Minab area.

According to sources, the US and Israel divided zones of responsibility: Israel targeted missile sites in western Iran, while the US targeted naval and missile assets in the south. (Reuters / Times of Israel)

Iranian authorities claim 165–180 dead, the majority girls aged 7–12. There is no independent confirmation of the exact figures. (CBS News, NBC News)

Iran and transparency: zero access

Fake #1: "Aircraft carrier on fire." The IRGC claimed it hit the USS Abraham Lincoln with ballistic missiles. Two videos instantly appeared on social media: an AI-generated clip of a burning carrier (incorrect deck markings, unrealistic details) and a clip from the video game Arma 3, re-uploaded with new captions. The videos racked up millions of views — including 10+ million on Chinese social media. US Central Command (CENTCOM) responded directly:

«Iran's IRGC claims to have struck USS Abraham Lincoln with ballistic missiles. LIE. The Lincoln was not hit. The missiles launched didn't even come close»

(Lead Stories, PolitiFact)

The key question that almost no one is asking: why should we believe Iranian propaganda at face value?

The Islamic Republic of Iran has never allowed an independent investigation into a single incident involving civilian casualties in its entire history:

  • 2025–2026 protests — total internet blackout, from 7,000 (HRANA) to 36,500 (leaked IRGC intelligence) killed, no independent investigation permitted
  • Flight PS752 — bulldozers on the crash site before investigators arrived, black boxes withheld for a year and a half
  • "Woman, Life, Freedom" protests (2022) — not a single independent observer admitted, bodies of the dead confiscated from families
  • November 2019 protests — internet shut down nationwide for a week, the death toll established only through leaks (Reuters: approximately 1,500)
  • 1988 mass executions — Iran denied the very fact for decades; the number of victims (up to 5,000 political prisoners) was established through survivor testimony and an audio recording of Ayatollah Montazeri

The playbook is always the same: deny → blame an external enemy → block access to the site → admit only under the pressure of irrefutable evidence. Flight PS752 is the textbook example: three days of lies, bulldozers on the wreckage, admission only after video footage of the missile launch surfaced.

In the case of Minab, the situation is no better. No independent investigator has gained physical access to the site. No international observers are present. Planet Labs satellite images were published through BBC Verify only on March 5 — five days after the strike; by that time bulldozers had already been working at the site. Iranian media published a handwritten list of 56 names — BBC Verify was able to verify only three. The primary source of casualty figures remains Iranian state institutions; the independent organization HRANA documents victims separately, but has no physical access to the site.

Where the school is — and what kind of school it is

Let's start with a detail that most media outlets preferred to ignore. The facility's full official name is "Shajareh Tayyebeh IRGC Navy Girls' School" — a girls' school belonging to the IRGC Navy. Not "a school near a military base." A school owned by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. According to anti-regime Iranian media — a school for the children of military personnel, built directly adjacent to the base.

Geolocation analysis conducted by independent researchers established that the school was located within the same fenced perimeter as the Sayyid al-Shuhada complex — the Asef Brigade headquarters. According to satellite imagery, until 2016 the building was located directly inside the military base; in September 2016 it was separated by an additional fence. (Wikipedia, geolocation / Ashkan Khatibi)

The school itself sits right up against the base perimeter — until 2016 it was actually inside that perimeter, then separated by a wall. The 600 meters everyone keeps citing as "far away" is the distance from the school to the Asef Brigade (Sayyid al-Shuhada) headquarters — i.e., to a specific military facility within the base. The base itself is roughly 500–700 meters long, a large complex with 8–10+ buildings (headquarters, clinic, pharmacy, cultural complex, etc.). In essence, the school is part of a cluster of military facilities.

CNN and The New York Times also confirmed: the building was a former military facility converted into a school, and historically was part of the IRGC base.

The picture on Google Maps is telling: the surrounding area is dense residential development. The school sits in the corner of a large rectangular plot with wide open spaces — the characteristic footprint of a military base. And you can clearly see that the school is separated from the residential area and sits right up against the military base.

Under strict Islamic canons, girls are not supposed to interact with unrelated men. That's precisely why the school is girls-only — there are no boys. Placing a girls' school right next to a military base housing hundreds of servicemen is, by Islamic standards themselves, a rather unconventional idea. But no one tells the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps what to do, so apparently it's fine.

Once more: until 2016, the school was literally inside the perimeter of the IRGC base — one fence, one complex. In 2016 it was separated by a wall, but the building remained right next to military facilities. CBC wrote directly that the school "was once part of" the IRGC base. So technically, by the time of the strike, the school was no longer "on" the base — but historically it was part of it, and it stands 600 meters from the missile brigade headquarters.

Important legal context: under Article 52 of Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions, civilian objects used for military purposes lose their protected status. And under Article 58, a party to a conflict is obligated to avoid placing military objectives near civilian populations. A school owned by the IRGC and located right next to a missile brigade headquarters is precisely such a case. The responsibility for placing children in a strike zone lies first and foremost with whoever put them there.

The IRGC in schools: a documented practice

The use of civilian infrastructure by Iranian security forces is not a hypothesis. It is a documented systemic practice in operation since the 1980s.

The Basij — the IRGC's militia wing — operates an organizational network of 40,000 to 54,000 bases (paygah), a significant portion of which are housed directly in school buildings across Iran. This is not wartime improvisation. This is a formalized structure built over decades. Child recruitment programs — "Omidan" (boys), "Puyandeqan" (girls), "Pishgaman" (teenagers) — operate through schools, involving students in ideological and paramilitary training from an early age. Schools and IRGC military infrastructure are not two separate worlds. They are structurally fused.

January 2026 — one month before the war began. Iranian citizens were spreading warnings on social media: don't send your children to school — the regime is turning them into Basij bases. (RightWingHeadlines)

The Iranian Teachers' Union officially warned about the growing presence of security forces in schools: Basij fighters, religious emissaries, plainclothes officers — entering school buildings across the country under various pretexts. (IranWire)

During the hostilities, video footage was captured showing IRGC and Basij units operating from school buildings in Tehran and Shiraz.

On March 3, IranIntl documented another revealing fact: Iran held an official press conference inside a school building — a demonstrative use of a civilian facility for information warfare purposes. (IranIntl)

Iran is not unique here — it exported this tactic. Hamas in Gaza adopted the same doctrine: in 2014, the UN agency for Palestinian refugees (UNRWA) discovered rockets hidden in its schools on three separate occasions. Three separate cases during a single operation. UNRWA officially condemned the use of its facilities and demanded explanations. Hezbollah in Lebanon followed the same model, placing launch platforms in residential neighborhoods and educational institutions. All of it — one school, in the literal sense: the IRGC's school, exported to its pupils. (Times of Israel)

This is not a minor detail. Under international humanitarian law, schools and hospitals lose their protected status the moment they are used for military purposes. The regime knows this — and that is precisely why it deliberately places military infrastructure in civilian facilities: so that when a strike comes, the world sees a school, not a missile base.

And now — the context in which all this happened. Negotiations concluded on February 26 in Geneva. At the first meeting, the Iranian delegation proudly announced: they had accumulated enough enriched uranium for 11 bombs, and all IAEA inspections had failed to prevent it. At the final meeting, the Americans offered a compromise — a 10-year moratorium on the nuclear program, no dismantlement, only IAEA oversight. Iran refused. Two days later, the bombing began — with two carrier strike groups in the region. Holding classes in a school adjacent to the IRGC missile brigade headquarters under those circumstances is either criminal negligence or a deliberate decision.

What happened at the scene

There are photographs of the destroyed building, some verified by independent sources. The building is small, destroyed. Open terrain surrounds it, with lots of broken brick.

Look carefully at what's happening around the site. A large number of people — almost exclusively men, without professional equipment. Heavy construction machinery is already operating: a bulldozer, an excavator, cranes. The footage was clearly not shot in the first minutes after the strike.

Meanwhile, in the early video footage: not a single ambulance, not a single doctor, not a single scene of medical assistance being provided. An important technical point: if there are living people under the rubble, you don't clear it with an excavator. A rescue operation is conducted by hand, carefully, with specialized equipment.

France's Le Monde noted this directly: "despite the large number of casualties, there is still little video footage showing them." I conducted research using Claude and Perplexity and could not find a single photograph of medical evacuation or first aid being administered. Checked ~60 images from Wikimedia Commons (18 files — ruins, rubble, rescuers working by hand, bodies), Getty Images (42 photos — ruins, funerals, bodies), Wikipedia, Al Jazeera, Reuters — nothing. The only textual mention of ambulances is on Wikipedia citing the NYT: "injured victims taken away in ambulances", but not a single photograph confirming this exists in the public domain. CNN and The Guardian publish as verified only the crowd and the destroyed building. (HonestReporting)

The funerals. March 3 — three days after the strike — Iranian state TV broadcast mass funerals with children's coffins draped in Islamic Republic flags. Hundreds of mourners in the streets, excavators preparing over 100 graves. It's important to understand: all footage, all figures, all names come exclusively from Iranian state propaganda. Aerial footage of the graves is Iranian; who is buried in them has not been independently confirmed. On top of that, a wave of fake AI-generated photographs of "mourners" and "children's coffins" swept through social media in the wake of the funerals — more on that below.

BBC published footage of the funerals but explicitly noted: "BBC News has not been able to independently verify the Iranian authorities' death toll". Iranian media published a handwritten list of 56 names with dates of birth — BBC Verify was able to cross-check only three of them against inscriptions on coffins in the video footage. Judicial authorities in Hormozgan province claimed to have identified 140 bodies — but this is data from Iranian officials, not independently confirmed.

A separate question is who exactly died. The Telegraph reported 165 victims, of whom only 81 were students. Who are the other 84 adults? Given that the school was on the grounds of an IRGC complex, some of the victims may have been military personnel or affiliated staff. Iran does not clarify this. At the same time, The Telegraph itself also relies exclusively on Iranian sources — there simply are no others. (HonestReporting)

Not a single foreign journalist was allowed access to the site. All Western investigations (NYT, BBC, CBC, NPR) were based on verification of Iranian videos and Planet Labs satellite imagery from March 4 — four days after the strike. Not a single international organization — the UN, ICRC, WHO — conducted its own investigation, limiting themselves to condemnations and calls for action. The death toll in Iranian statements kept climbing: first "over 40," then 153, then "over 160" — with a consistently low degree of independent verification.

How the propaganda machine worked

Iranian state TV issued a statement about children being killed. Al Jazeera immediately published a report, placing the blame on Israel. Western media picked it up — without asking a single question about the source. The "accusation → viral reach → belated fact-check" cycle worked flawlessly. (HonestReporting, Algemeiner)

The Iranian Embassy in Austria posted a photograph of a blood-stained school backpack on X — "the backpack of one of the dead girls." The post garnered 965,000 views, 8,000 reposts, 20,000 likes.

The photo went instantly viral. Al Jazeera English published a report placing the blame on Israel — 80,000 views. Western media followed: The Telegraph cited casualty figures referencing Iranian sources.

Researchers ran the backpack image through the SynthID detector. The result: a hidden Google Gemini watermark was found in the metadata. The photograph was AI-generated. The official embassy of a sovereign state was distributing an AI fake as evidence. (Tal Hagin / debunking, HonestReporting)

British MP Chris Hazzard (Sinn Féin) posted another AI-generated image of "mass funerals" on X with the caption "This image should be the front page of every news outlet" — 242,000 views, 7,000 reposts. The Hive Moderation detector rated the image as 100% AI-generated. (BOOM)

The question worth asking: why does the regime fabricate evidence of a tragedy if the tragedy is real? Genuine photographs of 168 dead children don't need to be processed through Google Gemini. Fabrications don't reinforce truth — they signal that the real picture doesn't match the one being claimed.

Fabricating casualties: this isn't the first time

Minab is not the first case where claimed casualty figures turned out to be massively inflated upon verification. There is a documented pattern.

2025–2026 Protests, Iran

The largest massacre in the history of the Islamic Republic — and the most recent example of how the regime handles numbers. Protests began on December 28, 2025, amid economic collapse. By January 8, 2026, they had spread to 675 locations across 210 cities in all 31 provinces of Iran.

On January 8, the regime imposed a total internet blackout — following the November 2019 playbook. The worst killings occurred under this blackout. The Iranian government claimed 3,117 deaths. The independent organization HRANA verified over 7,000 deaths with names and circumstances. Leaked IRGC intelligence reports, published by Iran International, cited 36,500. Time and The Guardian, citing a network of 80+ medical professionals inside the country, estimated approximately 30,000 killed in January 8–9 alone. Khamenei himself acknowledged "several thousand," calling all protesters "rioters and terrorists."

The spread of figures — from 3,117 (government) to 36,500 (IRGC leaks) — differs by a factor of 12. No independent international investigation was permitted. The UN Special Rapporteur stated the death toll "may surpass 20,000" and called for investigation of possible crimes against humanity. (Wikipedia, Wikipedia — 2026 Iran massacres)

This is the same regime that now asks the world to trust its figures about the school in Minab.

Al-Ahli Hospital, Gaza, October 2023

Hamas immediately claimed: an Israeli airstrike destroyed the hospital, 471 killed. Global media — including The New York Times — ran the figure in their headlines. Subsequent investigation (US intelligence, French intelligence, independent analysts) established: the strike was caused by a failed Islamic Jihad rocket, not an Israeli bomb; the hospital building was not destroyed — the parking lot was hit; the actual death toll was between 100 and 200, not 471. Neither Hamas nor Iran acknowledged responsibility, and the figure was never corrected. (Wikipedia, BBC Verify)

Jenin, April 2002

Palestinian sources and Arab media declared a "massacre" — up to 500 killed civilians. The term "genocide" was used widely. A UN commission that investigated the incident established: 52 Palestinians were killed, the majority of them armed combatants. Israel lost 23 soldiers — precisely because it fought with infantry in urban terrain instead of airstrikes, trying to minimize civilian casualties. (Wikipedia, Human Rights Watch)

Flight PS752, January 2020

The IRGC shot down a Ukrainian passenger plane — 176 killed. The Iranian regime categorically denied its involvement for three days, blaming a "technical malfunction." Bulldozers began clearing the crash site before international investigators arrived. The admission came only after Canada, Ukraine, and Bellingcat published irrefutable evidence — video footage of the missile launch and fragments of a Tor-M1 SAM warhead. (Wikipedia, Bellingcat)

Muhammad al-Durrah, September 2000

The most famous shot of the Second Intifada: a 12-year-old boy, allegedly killed by Israeli soldiers on camera for France 2. The footage became an icon — postage stamps, graffiti, calls for jihad. The problem is that the final seconds of the recording, where the "dead" boy lifts his hand from his face, were cut. An Israeli government investigation (the Kuperwasser Report, 2013) concluded: "not only was Muhammad not hit by IDF fire — he may not have been killed at all." Ballistic analysis showed that the Israeli position was at such an angle that bullets could not have made the holes in the wall behind the boy. The cameraman claimed 27 minutes of footage — only 18 were presented in court. A French appeals court in 2008 acquitted journalist Philippe Karsenty, who called the footage a "media hoax," ruling that he had "sufficient grounds" for the assertion. (Wikipedia, The Atlantic)

Qana, Lebanon, July 2006

Lebanese sources claimed: an Israeli strike killed 54 people, including 37 children. The Lebanese Red Cross and Human Rights Watch later established the actual number — 28 dead, 16 children. Nearly half of what was claimed. But what happened next was even more revealing: the head of civil defense in southern Lebanon, Salam Daher — later known as "Green Helmet" — was repeatedly photographed with the same body of a dead child in different poses, in different locations, for different film crews. To be clear: the child was really dead — the body was real. But Daher used it as a prop: pulling it out, repositioning it, carrying it elsewhere, letting another crew film it. This isn't a fake victim — it's staged footage using a real child's corpse for maximum media impact. Which is, arguably, even worse. (Wikipedia, HRW)

Iran and the Mina crush, September 2015

A crowd crush during the Hajj killed over 2,000 pilgrims — the deadliest Hajj disaster in history. Independent tallies: 2,411 (AP), 2,236 (AFP), 2,070 (Reuters). Saudi Arabia officially reported 769. Iran inflated the figure to 4,173 — taking the number from a Saudi Deputy Health Minister's webpage that was removed within three hours and disavowed as erroneous. Fars News even published a video walkthrough on how to access the cached page. Separately, Iran claimed that missing ambassador Roknabadi had been "kidnapped by Saudi Arabia" — his body was subsequently identified by his own brothers via DNA testing. (Wikipedia)

Mecca, July 1987

A clash between Iranian pilgrims and Saudi security forces. Iran's IRNA news agency announced: 400 Iranians killed, "several thousand" wounded. Actual figures: 402 dead total (275 Iranians, 85 Saudis, 42 from other countries), 649 wounded — not "several thousand." Iran presented the incident as an unprovoked massacre of peaceful pilgrims. According to U.S. intelligence and researcher Martin Kramer, the clash was mutual: Iranian pilgrims attempted to break through a security cordon, and violence came from both sides. Khomeini called for the overthrow of the Saudi monarchy; one million Iranians rallied at a "day of hatred" in Tehran. (Wikipedia)

"Red Cross under fire," Lebanon, July 2006

AP reported: "Israeli jets fired missiles at two Red Cross ambulances." The photographs went around the world. Detailed analysis showed: the damage to the vehicles was inconsistent with a missile strike — the metal was rusted (impossible from a fresh explosion), and the "entry hole" in the roof matched exactly with the standard ventilation hatch found on all vehicles of that model. Even Human Rights Watch subsequently acknowledged that the initial conclusion of a missile strike "was incorrect." (Wikipedia, Zombietime — full analysis)

The pattern is always the same: loud claim → instant amplification → belated fact-check → reality radically different from the initial figures. This machine has been running for decades. Minab is its latest product.

Preliminary conclusions

The reality is more complex than a simple "fake." The school existed. Children were probably inside — the strike hit on a Saturday, the first working day in Iran, around 10:45 AM. The strike was aimed at an IRGC military target — the headquarters of the IRGC Navy's Asef Missile Brigade. The school stood right next to the base perimeter, 600 meters from the headquarters.

What has been reliably established:

  • The school's full official name is "Shajareh Tayyebeh IRGC Navy Girls' School." The school belonged to the Revolutionary Guard Corps; according to anti-regime Iranian media, it was intended for the children of military personnel
  • Until 2016, the school was inside the perimeter of the IRGC base; it was then separated by a wall but remained adjacent to military facilities
  • The target of the strike was the IRGC Navy's Asef Missile Brigade headquarters, not the school. According to Reuters, American investigators are leaning toward the conclusion that the strike was carried out by US forces
  • The Iranian Embassy in Austria disseminated an AI-generated fake (a backpack) with a detectable Gemini watermark — 965,000 views
  • The IRGC and Basij systematically use school buildings as bases — up to 54,000 Basij bases in schools across Iran, a practice dating to the 1980s
  • Media repeated Iranian state claims without verification — the "accusation → viral reach → belated fact-check" cycle worked flawlessly
  • All previous cases (Al-Ahli, Jenin, Qana, Flight PS752) showed upon verification: initial figures inflated, the real picture radically different from what was claimed
  • Iran has never once in its entire history allowed an independent investigation into an incident involving civilian casualties

What remains independently unconfirmed:

  • The exact number of victims (165–180 according to Iranian data) — the sole source of figures is state TV. The Telegraph reported 165 victims, of whom only 81 were students. Who the other 84 are is unknown
  • A complete list of the dead — Iranian media published a handwritten list of 56 names; BBC Verify was able to cross-check only 3 of them. Judicial authorities claimed to have identified 140 bodies — but this is data from Iranian officials
  • Not a single international organization — the UN, ICRC, WHO — has conducted its own investigation
  • Not a single foreign journalist was allowed access to the site

A regime that places military headquarters next to schools, has used schools as militia bases for decades, systematically inflates casualty figures, blocks independent investigations, and then amplifies a real tragedy with AI fabrications — deserves not sympathy, but cold, documented analysis.

Information warfare: it's only going to get worse

Alongside the hot war, a full-scale information war is underway. And the situation will only get worse. None of us were taught information hygiene in school — and it's becoming a matter of survival.

To counter this, we need to employ something people have always had and no artificial intelligence can replace — source reputation.

Before absorbing information, trusting it, and especially sharing it — check where it came from.

People who repeat the talking points of deceitful propaganda are not victims. They are accomplices. Don't be an accomplice.

Conclusion

Use your head. Don't pick up information garbage off the floor — and certainly don't spread it further. Deliberate disinformation doesn't become truth through the number of reposts.

Source reputation is not an outdated concept. It is the only tool that works against the fake factory.
